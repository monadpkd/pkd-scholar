# Writing Style Guide

## Target Audience
Educated non-specialist reader. College-level: clear, analytical, evidence-based, readable. Not dumbed down, not jargon-dense.

## Tone
- Analytical, not devotional or fan-explanatory.
- Confident but not overclaiming.
- Direct, not hedging everything into mush.
- Comparative when possible — show how ideas connect across works.

## Sentence & Paragraph Style
- Lead with the claim, then provide evidence.
- Vary sentence length. Short sentences for emphasis. Longer ones for nuance.
- One idea per paragraph. Topic sentence first.
- Avoid throat-clearing ("It is interesting to note that...").
- Avoid redundant intensifiers ("very unique," "extremely important").

## Context Before Interpretation
- Define unfamiliar terms on first use, briefly and in-line.
- Give enough plot/work context for a reader who hasn't read the specific novel.
- But don't over-summarize — context serves the analytical point.

## Claims and Evidence
- State the claim. Cite the evidence. Explain why the evidence supports the claim.
- When evidence is ambiguous, say so. "Dick appears to..." not "Dick clearly..."
- When critics disagree, name the disagreement. Don't pick a winner unless the evidence is overwhelming.

## Expressing Uncertainty
- Use: "suggests," "appears to," "can be read as," "one interpretation is"
- Avoid: "clearly," "obviously," "undeniably," "it is certain that"
- When Dick contradicts himself (often): state both positions and note the tension.

## Comparing Texts
- Name both works, give dates, briefly describe the relevant element in each.
- State what the comparison reveals that neither work shows alone.
- Don't just list similarities — analyze what the pattern means.

## Evidentiary Layer Formulas
Use these to keep lanes separate:
- "In the fiction..."
- "In the Exegesis..."
- "In biographical criticism..."
- "In later scholarship..."
- "Dick self-describes this as..."
- "Critics have interpreted this as..."

## Banned Bad Habits
- "PKD clearly believed..." (unless evidence is direct and repeated)
- "This shows that Dick was [diagnosis]." (Don't diagnose.)
- "Interestingly," "Fascinatingly," "It is worth noting that" (throat-clearing)
- "In today's world of AI..." (anachronistic relevance-forcing)
- "Dick was ahead of his time" (hagiography)
- "This proves that..." (overclaiming from fiction)
- "As we all know..." (false consensus)
- Passive voice pileups
- Scare quotes around terms instead of defining them
- Citing a secondary source as if it were Dick's own words

## Templates

### Model Paragraph (Good)
"Paranoia in Dick's fiction functions not merely as pathology but as a cognitive method for registering hidden systems of control. In *Ubik*, Joe Chip's suspicion that reality is decaying around him turns out to be accurate: the environment is literally deteriorating under the influence of an antagonist operating within a simulated afterlife. The paranoid perception — that things are not what they seem and that unseen forces shape everyday experience — proves to be the most reliable epistemology available. In the Exegesis, Dick alternates between treating this pattern as evidence of genuine metaphysical insight and as a symptom of psychological disturbance, never fully resolving the tension (Exegesis, folder 49). Critics such as Fitting (1983) have argued that this irresolution is itself the point: paranoia in Dick is productive precisely because it refuses to settle into either clinical or mystical certainty."

### Model Paragraph (Bad — with commentary)
"Philip K. Dick was clearly a paranoid individual who projected his mental illness into his novels. His fascinating and groundbreaking works explore the nature of reality in ways that are incredibly relevant to today's AI-driven world. Interestingly, Ubik shows us that reality is not what it seems, which is something we can all relate to in the age of deepfakes. Dick was truly ahead of his time."

**What's wrong:** Diagnoses Dick. Uses "clearly," "fascinating," "incredibly," "interestingly." Forces anachronistic AI relevance. Hagiographic ("ahead of his time"). No citations. No evidentiary layers. No analytical claim.

### Short Entry Template
```
## [Topic Name]

**Definition.** [1-2 sentence scholarly definition in plain English.]

**PKD Relevance.** [Why this concept matters for understanding Dick's work. 2-3 sentences.]

**In the Fiction.** [Key appearances across works, with citations. Analytical, not plot summary.]

**In the Exegesis.** [How Dick theorizes or self-applies this concept. Citations to folders/dates.]

**Scholarly Debate.** [What critics say. Name specific scholars and positions.]

**Contradictions.** [Where Dick or the evidence pulls in incompatible directions.]

**See Also.** [Related topics] | [Key works] | [Key scholars]
```

### Longer Essay Template
```
## [Topic Name]: [Subtitle]

### Definition and Scope
[Scholarly definition. Boundaries of the concept. What it includes and excludes.]

### PKD Relevance
[Why this matters. How it connects to Dick's major concerns.]

### In the Fiction
[Work-by-work or chronological analysis. Brief quotes only when essential.]

### In the Exegesis
[Self-theorizing passages. Date-anchored when possible.]

### Intellectual Background
[Lane B context: what psychological/philosophical traditions inform this concept.]

### Scholarly Debate
[Lane C: what critics have argued. Named positions, not vague "scholars say."]

### Chronology
[How Dick's engagement with this concept changed over time.]

### Contradictions and Open Questions
[Unresolved tensions. Incompatible explanations. What we still don't know.]

### Key Passages
[Curated list: work, page/chapter, brief description of why each matters.]

### Related Entries
[Cross-links in three directions: neighboring concepts, major works, relevant scholars.]

### Editorial Note
[Confidence level. Known gaps. What would strengthen this entry.]
```
